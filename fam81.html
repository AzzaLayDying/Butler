Note on Marriage

Henry & Elizabeth arrived in Nelson on 21st April 1842 on the Bolton. They had leftr Gravesend on 29th Oct 1841 with 289 other passengers. All immigrants were selected and approved by I. Philpson. See extracts from "To Nelson by Saling Ship". (attached)On arrival they had 2 daughters: Martha aged 3 and Ann aged 18mo. They stayed in Nelson for a few years where 3 more daughters were born. Clarissa 1844, Lucy 1845 and Marion 1848. They went north to Russell where the rest of the children were born. Henry James 1851, Bertha 1853, Laura 1855, Arthur 1858, Harold 1861 making a family of 10. They moved between neighboring areas ofRussell and Waimate North. He set up a business in Russell as a cabinet maker, his Brass Name PLate is still in Pomalier House. It was found in the grounds of Pompalier House my MInistry of Works men covering a drain. It was cleaned and mounted on wood and now stands on the enclosed verandah upstairs. The Electoral Roll for 1855, 56, 57 show Henry Thomas Hickton as a baker and a freeholder.

On the waterfront of Russell still standsan old building named "The Gables", it was once owned by Henry (see story "Hanuted by the Past" Elizabeth is rememberedand still talked about by the descendants of the early settlers. She was the 1st school tteacher in Waimate North, a room in a house was 1st used then later a school room was built on (or maybe adjacent to) the 1st NZ flour Mill. An old building by the Waimate North Church was an early Sunday School and, some say< that that was the 1st school built in the area.

Elizabeth was a small frail women who kept her ringlets of hair under a lace cap. She ruled with an ebony cane, which kept even the biggest boys in order. She taught at the school for 14 yrs 1871 to 1885 and was farewelled in the Parsonage grounds on 26th Nov 1885. She had attended to her scholastic duties under the most trying of circumstances at times. A new school was built on a site of 5 acres in a central position and was running by JUne 1885 when the old school was broken up. The 1st school roll of the new school has been preserved and many of the families descendants remain in the arrea still. Among thos were Beatrice Barker and her brother Audie.

in 1883 "The TRavellkers REst" at Ohaewai was under license to H>R> Hickton, son of Henry and it is believed that Henry was living there at the time of his death in 1885. He used to bake bread there and the local maoris like to come into the bar and take the bread from the counter. Henry died 11th Oct 1885 and was buried in thechurch yard of ST John the Baptist Waimate North on Oct 13th: B;ock 11, grave No 33. HIs young son Arthur who had died at age 6yrs lies with him.

After his death Martha went to live IN North Shore with a married daughter (?Ann)

They were among the most respected pioneer settlers of the district and had brought up a largefamily who intermarried into the first families in the district. Elizabeth died in Devonport on March 11th 1900, she was 84 yrs and is bured at O'Neills Point, Devonport.

*taken from notes by Alleyne McCurdy (White).
