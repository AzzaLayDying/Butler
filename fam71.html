  • Frederick was a Captain who commanded various whaling

    ships out of Newfoundland in the USA. The master of a

    whaling ship was in charge of life and property and his word

    was law and where he willed, he could go. On his discretion

    and good judgement turned success or failure for all. No

    member of a whale ships crew, from the captain down,

    received fixed wages. Each man on board was paid a

    certain share (a 'lay') of the net result. The Master

    provisioned the ship in foreign ports with supplies and

    equipment, needing to be astute in these decisions as

    these expenses were deducted from the income generated

    by the boat in terms of whale oil and bone. Commonly a

    captain received a 'lay' ranging from a 10th to the 15th. What

    he got depending on how successful he had been in

    previous voyages and therefore how much in demand he

    was. In general a 'Bowhead' Whale was worth $10,000 and

    four big ones considered a good trip. The Arctic fleet sailed

    perpetually between the land and the off-shore ice, in some

    seasons working up the Siberian side, and others cruising

    to the eastwood of Point Barrow if ice permitted.

    conditions that Frederick experienced on his many voyages to the Arctic

Note on Husband: Frederick Alleyne BARKER (1)

On one occasion Captain Barkers ship was caught in the Arctic Ice pack and he and his crew were obliged to spend a long winter with the eskimos, entirely cut off from the outer world. At Russell, from whence he had sailed, he was given up for lost, but his wife refused to believe that he would not return. Every day she climbed Flagstaff Hill and scanned the horizen for an incoming sail. Her faith was rewarded when at the end of twelve months Captain Barker returned home, none the worse.

Note on Husband: Frederick Alleyne BARKER (4)

1869

The Whaling Industry

In their search for whales, American sailors eventually

covered the entire globe, in the process charting seas

which had previously been unknown to all but the

indigenous navigators. In the course of these voyages the

sailors stopped at almost every island, looking to restock

their supplies of food and fresh water, and even hiring new

crew from native population. They brought along goods to

trade for provisions and occasionally bartered as well for

souvenirs that would document their experiences when

they returned home. Today the artifacts that were collected

by sailors—and the descriptions and drawings that they

entered into their journals—are valued as representations of

cultural practices that, in many cases, are greatly altered or

extinct. American whalemen were thus both documenting

cultural changes and, unwittingly, contributing to them. The

exchange of technological knowledge and trade goods did

not go one way only. Sailors depended on the good will and

hospitality of the people they encountered along the way. In

the Arctic disasters of the 1870’s, American whalemen

owed their survival to the local people of northern Alaska,

who provided them with food and other support. Even in the

best of times ships were always in need of fresh water,

provisions, and firewood, and in the process of trading for

these supplies the ships encountered new information on

local waterways, stocks of fish and whales, and methods of

catching them.

Just then, to add to our horror, a huge wave swept over the

Our situation was now most critical, death truly stared us in

the face. But one chance remained for our lives and that

was to run the ship's bows on a few yards from where the

sea struck us, which I did. Another sea struck us and sent

us broadside on the beach. The beach was rocky and steep,

but all the crew managed to reach dry land in safety. I

remained below decks for three hours searching for

clothing, provisions and spirits for the relief of my men and

hoping that the vessel would meanwhile be washed higher

up on the beach. I watched my men struggling through the

surf, attempting to secure some things, and I finally swam

Note on Husband: Frederick Alleyne BARKER (12)

The Difficult Job of being the Master

Fred's log books reveal the extent of responsibility he bore. In the log from his voyage on the Mount Wollaston he reports on some of his medical interventions including dealing with sailors with hangovers, one (Charles Foster) who suffered from "epileptic fits, convulsions and vomiting" over several days (Rx emetic, salts, 3 Blue Pills, one (Mr Williams) with a broken leg that he splinted and built a 'box' to support, another who he had to amputate a finger, and tragically, caring for a sailor (who later died) who had his skull crushed by the boom of the ship. Besides his role as 'doctor', Fred deals with a variety of personnel issue including complaints about variety of food (beef, pork, flower, biscuit, beans, rice, potatoes, molasses and vinegar), insolence by a crew member to an officer. Then there are numerous maintenance and technical issues : a faulty chronometer and sextant, a filthy dirty rat infested ship which he fumigates by creating a smoky fire with charcoal, brimstone (sulphur which converts to sulphur dioxide when burnt) and blubber in the deck pot and then lowering it into the main hatch for 6 hours....followed later by sprinkling carbolic acid to disinfect and remove the odour of dead rats. leaky boat
