Note on Marriage

LifeSketch:William Crow was christened 2 January 1770 at St. Mary and St. Lawrence Parish Church in Great Waltham, Essex, England. William’s parents, William Crow and Anne Philebrown were married 3 January 1769. William was their oldest child. He had eight younger siblings—Anne b. 1771/ Hannah b. 1774/ Sarah b. 1776/ twins James and Charlotte b. 1778, both died in infancy/ Maria b. 1780/ John b. 1782/ and Jemima b. 1788. All the children were born in Great Waltham where it is believed the Crow Family had lived for several generations.

 

It is not known what brought William Crow to Stebbing, Essex, England. However, on 1 November 1792 William Crow married Elizabeth Boreham at St. Mary’s Parish Church, in Stebbing, Essex, England. The marriage record states that William Crow is “of this parish” and that Elizabeth Boreham is “of the parish of Shalford.” These statements are somewhat confusing as Elizabeth was born and raised in Stebbing and William was born and raised in Great Waltham. If this is the correct marriage record, then both William and Elizabeth had moved away from their hometowns to perhaps work for some amount of time.

 

William and Elizabeth Crow had a family of ten children: William b. 1793 who died as infant/ Betsey b. 1794/ William b. 1796 who died before age 2 / James b. 1798 / William b. 1802 / Charlotte b. 1805 / Rebecca b. 1807 / Joseph b 1810 / John b. 1812 / and Matilda b. 1815. All the children were born in Stebbing, Essex, England. Their first child was christened in St. Mary the Virgin Parish Church in Stebbing. The next two children were christened in the Mill End Independent Church at Stebbing. All the remaining children were eventually christened at the St. Mary The Virgin Parish Church, but some were already teenagers or older children by the time the christening took place.

 

William worked as a labourer to provide for his large family. His children grew up and went on to have families of their own.

William Crow passed away on 21 September 1840. His death certificate stated that he was 73 years old and that he died of dropsy. Dropsy is “an old term for the swelling of soft tissues due to the accumulation of excess water. In years gone by, a person might have been said to have dropsy. Today one would be more descriptive and specify the cause. Thus, the person might have edema due to congestive heart failure.” William Crow was buried 25 September 1840 at St. Mary the Virgin Parish Chursh, Stebbing, Essex, England. The burial record stated the William was 72 years old.

 

His wife, Elizabeth Crow, remained a widow until her death about a decade later.
